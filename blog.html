Philosophy April 2026

Peace is Not the Absence of War: It is Freedom from Climate Worry

Peace is a state of complete physical, mental, and social well-being. As far as I’m concerned, there’s nothing like social justice without environmental justice. Millions of youth globally don’t dream about peace; they dream about doom. They are mentally too tired to be present.

Real peace is freedom from climate change because the opposite of peace isn’t war—it is apprehensiveness. Eco-anxiety affects community well-being through the loss of social identity, cohesion, and interpersonal aggression. At ECO-HOPE, we believe the path to peace starts with healing our relationship with the planet.

Origin Story April 2026

Hacksaw Ridge and the Seed of Hope

In my final year of high school, I skipped an entrepreneurship test to watch movies. I saw Desmond Doss in Hacksaw Ridge utter words that stayed with me for eight years: "While everybody is taking life, I'm going to be saving it."

Against that background, I wanted to challenge the apocalyptic narratives of the climate crisis. ECO-HOPE was born not just as a project, but as a movement of healing, learning, and action. Hope is the seed we plant today so children can inherit a tomorrow worth living.

Climate Action March 2026

Mindsets Over Bins: The Kampala Waste Future

When you arrive in Kampala, you see solid waste scattered everywhere—plastics and debris clogging our waterways. But a waste-free future starts with a change of mindset, and that is where ECO-HOPE begins.

Children between the ages of 3 and 12 are at the heart of this transformation. They are still shaping their values. What we plant in their minds today will define the Uganda they inherit tomorrow. By educating them now, we shape a generation ready to protect the planet.

Storytelling February 2026

The Ancient Technology: Storytelling

"The one who tells the story, rules the world." Stories are an ancient technology that help us guide our reality. Most of our communication happens through anecdotes rather than graphics and statistics.

Relatable human stories shift climate change from a scientific to a social reality. At ECO-HOPE, we use positive language to wrap facts in emotion, making climate engagement accessible, persuasive, and memorable for the youth.

Founder's Vision

JOHN KAMULEGEYA, Founder – ECO-HOPE Initiative

Maya Angelou, the great poet, once said, "There is no greater agony than bearing an untold story inside you." In her 1969 autobiography I Know Why the Caged Bird Sings. There's one thing I truly believe, young people have a caboodle of stories to tell. Change often begins with one conversation, one story, one idea.

In a world where children and youth often feel overwhelmed by the weight of climate change, ECO-HOPE was born—not just as a project, but as a movement of healing, learning, and action. Because both of us can see eye to eye that hope is not passive. Hope is action. Hope is a story that inspires courage. Hope is the seed we plant today so that children can inherit a tomorrow worth living.

At its heart, ECO-HOPE is about transforming climate anxiety into climate possibility. We use the power of storytelling to give voice to the fears and dreams of young people, climate education to equip them with knowledge and resilience, and climate action to turn their hope into tangible change – through tree planting, school clubs, and community projects.

At ECO-HOPE, we believe climate action starts with climate understanding. By educating children today, we shape informed citizens, stronger communities and a generation ready to protect the planet.

NAMWANJE BENITAH, 14 YRS

"Water needs us to survive"

Water pollution feels personal because women and girls fetch water most. My fear grows when lakes are used as dumping pits, when rivers turn black with waste, when oceans burn with oil spills, when wells smell of chemicals. But solutions exist, and they must start with us: Eco-education in schools and public spaces. Today I swam to make the world listen. Tomorrow, we swim into action. So remember this: We don't just need water to live. Water needs us to survive.

KIWALABYE RASHID

"Caring for the Earth is Amanah from Allah"

Climate change is real, and many of our young people are growing up with fear and uncertainty. But Islam teaches us something powerful: we do not respond to fear with despair – we respond with action and faith. Planting a tree is an act of ongoing charity (Sadaqah Jariyah); Preserving nature is an act of gratitude; Teaching our children to care for the Earth is an act of leadership. Let our mosques become centers not only of prayer – but of environmental responsibility, awareness, and hope.

AMUTAHAIRE KAHIMA

Young Writer & Literature Student at MUK

What Gives me hope? The growing number of young people who are becoming involved in environmental action. When many people work together, more actions create powerful change. The more people understand the causes and effects of climate change, the more they make informed choices about how they live and care for the environment. Climate hope reminds us that people still have the power to change.

SWABRA MANDELA NAKALANZI

Masters Climate Change Science, Makerere

What gives me climate hope today: My experience studying Climate Change at Makerere University showed me how knowledge can empower action. When people understand the signs of climate change and pathways for solutions, they become confident in contributing to change through research, advocacy, or community initiatives. Education transforms climate anxiety into agency.

FORTUNE SSEWANKAMBO

Artist & Climate Storyteller

"Through art, I turn pain into pictures that people can see, feel, and understand." This is my neighborhood when it flooded. A drawing of a flooded home can speak louder than statistics. When we use art, we don't just express what climate change is doing to us – we remind the world why it must stop.

TAMALE JEREMIAH

"The Earth needs voices that can sing"

Music allows us to express what words alone cannot carry. It turns eco-anxiety into sound, pain into performance, and silence into a message the world can hear. Through songs, drama, and performance, we don't just tell people about climate change – we make them feel it. And when people feel, they begin to care. When they care, they begin to act.
